Skulls and Portraiture

These are photos of studies from a concertina I made at the start of my skulls project. In order to start designing a possible final piece I wanted to first establish what media to use. To begin with I experimented with using different materials on their own E.g. oil pastel, watercolor and acrylic paint.

As I worked through the concertina I decided to start combining techniques to create a mixed media piece. I found this worked really well when I wanted to start establishing the differences between textures of the skull and other elements of the piece, like smoke. For example, I used the piece to the right as a way of testing out how biro and oil pastel may look layered on top of watercolour. This was a successful experiment as I liked how the different toned watercolor created atmospheric perspective. In addition a more interesting composition was created when I added elements like smoke or “confetti” (embroidered piece to the left) to the piece. I was able to play around with how this element interacted with the blank space surrounding the skull.

The two images above are sustained pieces from my skulls project. The skull on the left was created by layering blue biro over a watercolour wash which I painted on to help build up the darkest areas. I then decided to use orange for the smoke to create contrast and sharpen the borders between the highly rendered skull and simple lines in the smoke. The skull to the left is a tonal study.

The mood board to the right contains a variety of different artist’s take on portraiture. I chose to draw inspiration from these artists works as I wanted to focus on distorting or hiding sections of the face.

To the left are two pages of my Y12 fine art sketchbook containing some reference photos I took to work from when creating studies. When taking the photos on the second page I decided to experiment with taking blurred photographs in order to create a distorted look similar to the portraits on my Mood board.

To the right are a series of artists researches and studies where I experimented with layering acrylic paint. I experimented with using both controlled and expressive brush strokes. Ultimately I decided to favour more expressive brush strokes as I thought later on in the project I could use this to create a similar effect to my blurred photographs.

The above images formed part of my experimentation and research into portraiture in preparation for creating a final piece where I combine skulls and the human form.

The image on the left is the planning page I created when I was generating the final composition of my portrait that combined my investigations into skulls and portraiture. I used Procreate to combine my reference images of skulls and a self portrait. I used brush tools in order to mimic how a painted piece my look when loosely overlapping with a tonal drawing.

To the right is the final piece that resulted from my skulls and portraiture project. Like practiced during my portraiture studies I used more expressive brush strokes when painting the portrait instead of small blended brush strokes. I did this as I thought the expressive brush strokes would make the borders between the painting and tonal drawing look better. I was also inspired by Elly Smallwood to leave some areas unpainted as well as leaving the grid used to draw the compositions outline showing. This combined with the skull allowed me to create a somewhat distorted rendition of a self portrait.
